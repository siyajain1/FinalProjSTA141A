  1. ABSTRACT

This project dives into 18 sessions of experiments involving 10 mice from Steinmetz et al. (2019), focusing on mice navigating visual discrimination tasks. Each session is packed with trials featuring varied contrast stimuli and corresponding neural activity recordings. Exploratory data analysis of the whole data shows trends in spike rates across sessions and identifies brain areas consistently active throughout trials.   1. INTRODUCTION

The understanding of neural mechanisms underlying decision-making processes has been a central pursuit in neuroscience research. In the quest to unravel these intricate processes, studies like that of Steinmetz et al. (2019) have provided invaluable insights into the neural correlates of decision-making in rodents. This project aims to delve deeper into the rich dataset generated by Steinmetz et al., focusing on a subset of experiments conducted on mice engaged in visual discrimination tasks.

Steinmetz et al. conducted experiments involving 10 mice over 39 sessions, each comprising numerous trials where visual stimuli of varying contrast levels were presented. The mice, tasked with making decisions based on these stimuli, exhibited distinct behavioral responses, which were further influenced by subsequent feedback administration. The neural activity of the mice's visual cortex during these trials was recorded in the form of spike trains, capturing the precise timing of neuron firings.

For this project, 18 of the 39 sessions are used in which there are varying amounts of hundreds of trials within each trial. Each trial consists of 5 variables: feedback_type: type of the feedback, 1 for success and -1 for failure

  • contrast_left: contrast of the left stimulus
  • contrast_right: contrast of the right stimulus
  • time: centers of the time bins for spks
  • spks: numbers of spikes of neurons in the visual cortex in time bins defined in time
  • brain_area: area of the brain where each neuron lives

The primary objective of this project is to construct a predictive model to anticipate the outcome of each trial, i.e., the type of feedback received by the mice, utilizing the neural activity data alongside stimulus conditions.

 Since average spikes had similar patterns across sessions regardless of brain area, I decided to add the average spike rate of each trial in each session. My final data frame represented each trial as a row with variables of feedback type, contrast difference (left contrast and right contrast), trial ID, session ID, mouse name, average spike rate across the whole trial, and decision type. The four levels of the decision class were decided where (1) the left contrast was greater than right contrast, (2) the right contrast was greater than left contrast, (3) both left and right were 0, or (4) both left and right were equal but non-zeros. I created a function to create a summary of all the trials over one session, and then concatenated all the session summaries into one large dataset (data_tibble).

  1. PREDICTIVE MODELING

There are three main types of predictive modeling that I attempted- Logistic Regression, K-Nearest Neighbors, and Gradient Boosting. Over all of the models, I decided to ultimately use the contrast difference and average spike rate for the trial since decision type correlates with the difference of the left and right contrast. Since the contrast difference was different for each trial, it was considered as a factor, and each of the models gave a weight to each level in the contrast difference variable.

In the summary of the logistic regression model, we can see that all of the variables are significant in predicting feedback type except for the contrast difference level of 0.25, and the contrast difference level of 0 wasn’t even included in the logistic regression model. This means no weight was given to that contrast difference level, and even for the 0.25 level, it has one of the lowest weights of 0.182.  This could possibly be because as the differences between the two variables decrease, the outcome of the feedback type is more randomized rather than it consistently being a success or not a success. Overall, the logistic regression model gave an overall accuracy of 71.09%. 

K-Nearest Neighbor (k = 10)

After trying multiple values for k- nearest neighbors, I saw that k = 10 gave the best model performance. The confusion matrix from the KNN model showed that the model classified more of the positive class as negative than positive, but had a high true negative predictive value where -1 is the positive class and 1 is the negative class. There is a high specificity rate for the KNN model since it accurately predicted 95.99% of the negative instances as negative. The overall accuracy was 71.68% however, there was a low recall and F1 score, indicating that the model had issues with correctly identifying positive instances. As we can see from this, the KNN model is not a high performing model on this data.

Gradient Boosting

The gradient boosting model was my third attempt at creating a predictive model with the same features, so even if there are many weaker smaller trees, the gradient boosting would combine them to create the most efficient and accurate model for the data. I set the objective to binary logistic regression since the data has a binary output. However, I had to convert all the data to the right format for the xgboost model building function in R since the train and test labels were not in a numeric value. After training it with 10 iterations, the loss decreased overall from 0.578118 to 0.557778 which is a good indicative measure that the model is able to minimize the loss in each step of training the model. The model had an overall accuracy of 70.64%.

The confusion matrix shows that this model had the same issue as the KNN model where it had a high rate of accurately predicting the negative class which represents the successes, but a low true positive rate.
